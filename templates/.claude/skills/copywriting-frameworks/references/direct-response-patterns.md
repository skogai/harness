## Source Note

These patterns are distilled and paraphrased from a user-provided transcript of a copywriting discussion with Sam Parr, combined with general direct-response practice. Use them as reusable heuristics, not as quoted source material.

## Core Model

- Copywriting is behavior design through language: get the right person to keep reading, believe, and act.
- The goal is not universal appeal. The goal is high conversion among the right audience.
- Every sentence has one job: make the next sentence feel worth reading.
- Long copy can work when it keeps creating tension, proof, or desire. Boring copy fails at any length.
- The product should often arrive after desire is built, not in the opening sentence.

## AIDA Pass

Use AIDA when writing ads, landing pages, sales emails, scripts, or offer explanations.

1. Attention: make the reader stop with a specific problem, identity, claim, contradiction, image, or curiosity gap.
2. Interest: show that you understand the reader's lived situation better than generic competitors do.
3. Desire: explain the mechanism, transformation, proof, and emotional payoff.
4. Action: make the next step clear, low-friction, and consistent with the promise.

## Hook Patterns

Use one or combine two.

- Identity targeting: call out a precise audience, status, place, stage, or aspiration.
- Curiosity gap: create a question the reader feels pressure to resolve.
- Proof first: lead with before/after, screenshots, retention data, testimonials, demos, or a vivid customer quote.
- Visual analogy: translate an abstract claim into a familiar picture, comparison, or object.
- Failed solution: name what the reader tried, why it failed, and why that failure was not a character flaw.
- Known phrase twist: bend a familiar phrase so it feels recognizable but fresh.
- Objection first: name the skepticism before the reader does.
- Status desire: speak to the person the reader wants to become, not just the problem they want removed.
- Time constraint: promise a useful idea quickly before expanding into the full argument.

## Rewrite Moves

When copy is generic, replace feature-first language with a reader-first sequence:

1. Start from the customer moment, not the product category.
2. Name the frustrating pattern in plain language.
3. Explain the mechanism behind the problem.
4. Add proof or mark the needed proof.
5. Introduce the product as the simplest credible fix.
6. Handle the strongest objection.
7. Ask for the next action.

Useful template:

```text
If you are [specific person] and [specific problem] keeps happening, it may not be because [blame or failed solution]. The real issue is [mechanism]. [Proof or needs proof]. That is why [product] helps you [transformation] without [objection]. [CTA].
```

## Story Structure

Use story when the offer needs belief, emotional stakes, or differentiation.

- Make the customer the hero.
- Make the brand, product, community, or content the guide.
- Add tension: before/after, but/therefore turns, false starts, stakes, and a moment of realization.
- Use concrete scenes instead of abstract claims.
- Let the CTA feel like the next step in the story, not an interruption.

For founder stories, avoid autobiography for its own sake. Use the founder's experience only to prove why the product exists, why the customer problem matters, or why the company has unusual insight.

## Objection Handling

List likely objections before writing:

- Why should I trust this?
- Why now?
- Why is this different from what I already tried?
- Why is the price fair?
- Will this work for someone like me?
- What tradeoff am I worried about?

Handle objections through proof, story, demonstration, comparison, FAQ, or a concrete anecdote. Do not rely only on reassurance.

## Price and Desire

Do not lead with discounts unless the audience is already high-intent. Build desire first, then make the price feel like access, relief, leverage, or a surprisingly reasonable exchange.

## Style Rules

- Write at a simple reading level unless the brand explicitly requires technical density.
- Use one main point per sentence.
- Replace many commas with periods.
- Vary rhythm: short, medium, long, short.
- Use `and` or `but` at the start of a sentence when it improves flow.
- Cut the first draft aggressively.
- Avoid weak openings like `Introducing`, `We are excited`, or broad category claims.
- Use humor only when the brand has permission to sound funny.

## Critique Rubric

Ask these questions in order:

1. Who exactly is this for?
2. Does the first line create enough pressure to read the second?
3. Is the promise specific, visual, and emotionally legible?
4. Is there proof, or only assertion?
5. Does the copy explain why the problem exists?
6. Does it build desire before pitching the product?
7. Are the strongest objections handled?
8. Is the CTA clear and proportional to the ask?
9. Could 30 percent of the words be cut without losing persuasion?

## Copywork Routine

Use copywork when the user asks to improve taste, build a swipe file, or learn a medium.

1. Select 5-20 proven examples from the target medium: ads for ads, scripts for scripts, landing pages for landing pages.
2. Copy each example by hand or retype it exactly.
3. Annotate the hook, audience selection, transitions, proof, tension, objections, and CTA.
4. If performance data exists, mark where attention drops and where new loops or proof appear.
5. Extract reusable structures, not protected phrasing, claims, or brand identity.
6. Write new variants from the structure using the user's audience, offer, and proof.

Look outside the user's category for fresh language: magazines, fiction, stand-up, old ads, niche communities, founder letters, and high-performing social scripts.
